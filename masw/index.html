Omaha sits on a complex geological foundation shaped by the Missouri River and thick glacial deposits, making site classification under ASCE 7-22 and the International Building Code a critical first step for any structural project. The city's eastern neighborhoods rest on deep alluvial silts and clays that can amplify seismic waves, while the loess-covered bluffs to the west present a different dynamic response entirely. The seismic refraction survey method offers a complementary approach when bedrock depth is uncertain, but for direct measurement of shear wave velocity, the Multichannel Analysis of Surface Waves provides the most reliable VS30 profile. Our field crew deploys a 24-channel seismograph with 4.5 Hz geophones across sites from Downtown to Millard, recording Rayleigh wave dispersion to map stiffness variations down to 30 meters. The resulting data feeds directly into the seismic design parameters that structural engineers need for base shear calculations and site-specific response spectra.

A site class boundary shift—from D to E, or C to D—can change seismic design forces by over 40 percent, making direct VS30 measurement far more economical than conservative assumptions.

Scope of work

The test array uses a 48-channel Geometrics Geode seismograph coupled with low-frequency geophones spaced at 2-meter intervals, energized by a 10-kg sledgehammer source on an aluminum strike plate. This setup generates surface waves in the 5 to 50 Hz range, which the inversion software processes through frequency-wavenumber transformation to extract the fundamental-mode dispersion curve. Once the shear wave velocity profile is inverted, the travel-time average to 30 meters yields the VS30 value that places the site into an ASCE 7 Site Class—from stiff soil profiles typical of the Dundee area to softer deposits near Carter Lake. For projects requiring deep foundation design in the Missouri River floodplain, the VS30 alone is not sufficient; our engineers correlate the surface wave results with CPT soundings to verify the velocity profile against tip resistance and sleeve friction data, ensuring the site classification holds up under peer review and plan check.

Area-specific notes

Much of Omaha’s mid-century expansion—particularly the subdivisions built west of 72nd Street in the 1950s and 1960s—proceeded without the benefit of modern shear wave velocity testing, leaving a legacy of structures whose seismic resilience is poorly understood. The combination of deep loess deposits, variable groundwater levels tied to the Ogallala aquifer recharge, and low-strain stiffness contrasts between natural soil and engineered fill creates conditions where site amplification can be significantly underestimated by generic code defaults. A site near the Missouri River that maps to Site Class D using only blow-count correlations may, in fact, fall into Site Class E when measured with MASW, triggering stricter detailing requirements for the lateral force-resisting system. Retrofitting an existing building after discovering a misclassification is vastly more expensive than conducting the survey before finalizing foundation elevations.

Linked services

01

Standard VS30 Site Classification

A single linear array deployed at the building footprint, processed to yield a 1D shear wave velocity profile and the corresponding ASCE 7 Site Class letter. Suitable for structures under 160 feet in height where a simplified lateral force procedure is acceptable.

02

2D Shear Wave Velocity Cross-Sections

Multiple parallel MASW lines spaced across the project parcel, stitched into a 2D velocity model. This is used for large-footprint buildings, cut-and-cover excavations, or sites where the bedrock surface is known to dip sharply across the property.

03

Site-Specific Response Spectra

When the IBC requires a ground motion hazard analysis—common for Risk Category III and IV structures in Omaha—we combine the measured VS30 profile with probabilistic seismic hazard data from the USGS Unified Hazard Tool to generate design response spectra that can reduce base shear demands compared to code defaults.

Q&A

How much does a MASW / VS30 survey cost for a typical commercial lot in Omaha?

For a single-array MASW survey on a standard commercial parcel in the Omaha metro, the cost typically ranges from US$1.740 to US$2.840, depending on site access, line length, and the level of reporting detail required. A site where we can deploy a single 48-channel spread without clearing obstacles will be at the lower end; a site requiring multiple arrays, brush removal, or traffic control will trend toward the upper end of that range.

What site class does Omaha usually fall into?

There is no single answer for the entire city. The Missouri River floodplain and areas near Carter Lake often fall into Site Class D or E due to deep soft alluvium. The loess-covered uplands west of downtown—around Westside, Aksarben, and Millard—typically range from Site Class C to D, but local variations in loess thickness and groundwater can shift a site across a class boundary. Direct measurement is the only way to know for certain.

How long does a MASW test take on site?

A single MASW line with a 48-channel array takes about 45 to 90 minutes of field time, including setup, multiple source impacts for stacking, and breakdown. A site with two or three lines plus a topographic survey for the report can be completed in half a day. Processing and report writing typically require an additional three to five business days.

Do you need to drill boreholes in addition to the MASW survey?

MASW measures shear wave velocity directly without drilling, but the best geotechnical models in Omaha combine surface wave data with borehole logs. When we have an SPT or CPT profile at the same location, we can calibrate the velocity inversion and identify exactly which stratigraphic units correspond to which velocity layers. This is especially useful for liquefaction screening and for convincing plan reviewers who want multiple lines of evidence.

Can MASW be used on a site with buried utilities?

Yes, provided we know the utility locations in advance. The geophone line can be offset or aligned to avoid underground obstructions, and the surface wave method is non-invasive enough that it will not damage pipes or conduits. We coordinate with Nebraska 811 for utility locating before any fieldwork begins.
