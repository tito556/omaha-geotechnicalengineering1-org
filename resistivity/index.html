When our crew rolls out the multielectrode cable and sets the four stainless steel stakes into the glacial till, the ABEM Terrameter starts logging apparent resistivity values within minutes. In Omaha, we rely on electrical resistivity surveys to cut through the guesswork of hidden subsurface conditions. The Missouri River shaped these terraces and filled paleochannels with silty clay over limestone, and a single boring rarely tells the whole story. That is where a vertical electrical sounding campaign pays off. We run Schlumberger and dipole-dipole arrays across the site, adjusting maximum AB/2 spacing to reach depths of 100 feet or more when we need to confirm bedrock competency under proposed mat foundations. The output is a 2D resistivity profile that highlights sand lenses, perched water, or weathered shale layers which standard test pits might miss entirely.

Resistivity imaging in the Missouri River valley reveals paleochannels that single-point borings cannot detect, saving foundation redesign costs.

Scope of work

Omaha’s eastward expansion from the Old Market toward Carter Lake forced developers to build on former floodplain deposits where fill thickness varies by 30 feet in a single block. That history left a patchwork of compressible clays and old channel sands that create real headaches for deep excavations. Running VES lines before drilling lets our team optimize boring locations and avoid hitting a buried sand seam during a CPT sounding that skews friction ratio readings. We invert the field data with Res2DInv software, generating RMS errors below 5% on most profiles, and we cross-check the geoelectric layers against USCS soil classifications from nearby borings. For stormwater retention basins, we also map low-resistivity zones that indicate clay-rich aquitards, which is critical for meeting Omaha’s post-2008 drainage ordinance requirements.

Area-specific notes

In Omaha’s older industrial corridors south of I-80, we often encounter buried metallic debris, slag fill, or abandoned utilities that create conductive noise in the raw data. If you skip a pre-survey utility clearance and desktop review of historical Sanborn maps, you end up with inverted resistivity sections showing phantom low-resistivity anomalies that look like groundwater but are actually a buried rail spur. Our field protocol includes a 60 Hz noise monitor step and repeating short segments with different array geometries to identify cultural interference. We also flag areas where the high clay content of the Peoria Loess saturates below 15 ohm-m, masking subtle sand-gravel contacts. Without that local calibration, a contractor risks placing footings on compressible lenses that go undetected until differential settlement appears.

Linked services

01

1D Vertical Electrical Soundings (VES)

Point soundings using Schlumberger array with expanding AB/2 spacing. We deliver interpreted layer thickness and resistivity values calibrated to Omaha-area USCS soil types, ideal for foundation design on glacial till over Pennsylvanian limestone.

02

2D Electrical Resistivity Tomography (ERT)

Multi-electrode profiles (28 to 64 electrodes) producing continuous resistivity cross-sections. We target paleochannel mapping, levee condition assessment, and delineation of fill thickness for redevelopment sites near the Missouri River.

03

Cross-Borehole Resistivity Tomography

Electrode strings deployed in PVC-cased CPT holes or monitoring wells. This technique images inter-borehole lithology with submeter resolution, used on deep excavation projects where vertical anomalies would be missed by surface arrays alone.

Q&A

How much does a resistivity survey cost for a typical Omaha commercial lot?

For a standard 1D VES sounding or a short 2D profile on a commercial lot around Omaha, budget between US$660 and US$1,060. The final figure depends on the array length, number of electrodes, and whether we need to mobilize an extra technician for traffic control along Douglas or Sarpy County roads.

Can resistivity tell the difference between saturated sand and clay in the Missouri River floodplain?

Yes, usually. Clean saturated sands in the Missouri alluvium typically read above 50 ohm-m, while the fat clays of the Peoria Loess and alluvial slackwater deposits fall below 20 ohm-m. We always calibrate the resistivity ranges against at least one boring log on site because clayey sand can produce overlapping values. When the distinction is critical, we pair resistivity with a few CPT soundings to break the ambiguity with sleeve friction data.

What site conditions in Omaha limit the effectiveness of electrical resistivity?

Three local conditions reduce data quality: areas with thick slag or cinder fill from old rail yards near South Omaha, sites paved with heavily reinforced concrete where electrode contact resistance spikes, and urban corridors with active 60 Hz ground currents from buried power lines. We mitigate these by wetting electrode contacts with bentonite slurry, using longer staking distances for Wenner arrays to push current below the noise, and running reciprocal measurements to filter bad data points.
